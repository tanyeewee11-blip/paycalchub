What Does $15 an Hour Mean in Real Life?

$15 an hour is the $15/hour minimum wage target adopted by many US states. At full-time hours (40/week), this translates to $31,200 per year before taxes, or approximately $27,108 after federal taxes for a single filer — giving you roughly $2,259 per month to budget with.

With careful budgeting, $15/hour covers basic living costs in most mid-size US cities. Sharing accommodation is common at this income level.

Jobs That Pay $15 an Hour

Common roles at or near this rate include: retail cashier, food service worker, warehouse associate, home health aide, customer service representative. Wages vary significantly by location, experience, and employer — use our Salary Calculator to see your actual take-home after state taxes.

How to Calculate $15/Hour to Annual Salary

The formula is: $15 × 40 hours × 52 weeks = $31,200. This assumes a standard full-time schedule with no unpaid time off. If you take 2 weeks unpaid leave, the adjusted annual figure is $30,000. Use our Hourly to Salary Calculator to model any hours and weeks combination.

Frequently Asked Questions

$15 an hour is how much a year? +
$15 an hour equals $31,200 per year before taxes, assuming 40 hours per week for 52 weeks (2,080 hours). If you work 50 weeks (taking 2 weeks unpaid), that drops to $30,000.
What is $15 an hour after taxes? +
For a single filer, $15/hour ($31,200/year) nets approximately $27,108 after federal income tax and FICA. That’s about $2,259/month. State taxes will reduce this further — see our Salary Calculator for state-by-state results.
How much is $15/hour per month? +
$15 an hour works out to $2,600 gross per month ($31,200 ÷ 12). After federal taxes, take-home is approximately $2,259 per month for a single filer.
What is the bi-weekly paycheck for $15/hour? +
At $15/hour working 40 hours per week, your gross bi-weekly pay is $1,200 (80 hours × $15). After federal taxes, the estimated net bi-weekly paycheck is approximately $1,043.
Is $15 an hour a good wage? +
$31,200/year puts you below the US median individual income of roughly $45,000. It covers basic expenses in most areas but budget discipline is important. Cost of living varies greatly by location.
