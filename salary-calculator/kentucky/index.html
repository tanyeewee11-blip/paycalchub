Kentucky Income Tax Overview (2026)

Kentucky has a 4.0% flat income tax rate as of 2024, reduced from 4.5%. Kentucky plans further reductions depending on revenue conditions. Local occupational taxes may also apply in some counties and cities.

In addition to state tax, all Kentucky workers pay federal income tax (10%–37% marginal rates) and FICA — Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%), totaling 7.65% of gross wages. Voluntary deductions like 401(k) contributions or health insurance premiums further reduce taxable income.

Cost of Living in Kentucky

Kentucky is one of the most affordable states. Louisville median home prices are around $230,000. Lexington is slightly more expensive. One-bedrooms in Louisville average $950/month.

When evaluating a job offer in Kentucky, consider not just the salary but total compensation — employer benefits, retirement match, and location-specific costs. A $70,000 salary in a low cost-of-living Kentucky city may be more livable than $85,000 in a high-cost metro.

Top Industries and Employers in Kentucky

Kentucky's economy is anchored by automotive manufacturing (Toyota, Ford), healthcare, logistics, bourbon production, horse racing. The state median household income is approximately $52,000 per year, though this varies significantly by region and occupation.

Kentucky follows the federal minimum wage floor of $7.25/hour, so entry-level pay here is set largely by employer competition rather than state law, and the state's $52,000 median household income sits close to the national average, with automotive manufacturing as the largest single influence on local pay scales.

Kentucky's 4.0% flat rate means a worker earning $75,000 pays approximately $3,000 in state income tax annually. Compared to zero-tax states, this reduces take-home by $3,000/year — a meaningful difference that compounds significantly over a career.

How to Calculate Your Kentucky Take-Home Pay

Your net pay is your gross salary minus: (1) federal income tax based on your bracket and filing status, (2) FICA at 7.65% of gross wages, (3) Kentucky state income tax at 4.0%, and (4) any voluntary deductions such as 401(k), HSA, or health insurance premiums. The calculator above handles steps 1–3 automatically.

Tax-Advantaged Strategies for Kentucky Workers

Regardless of your state tax rate, contributing to pre-tax retirement accounts like a 401(k) or traditional IRA reduces your federal taxable income dollar for dollar. In 2026, you can contribute up to $23,500 to a 401(k) and $7,000 to an IRA. For someone earning $60,000 in Kentucky, maxing the 401(k) reduces federal taxable income by $23,500 — saving approximately $2,820 in federal tax (at 12% marginal rate) plus approximately $940 in Kentucky state tax.
