Colorado Salary After Tax 2026

State Tax Guide January 2026 8 min read

Colorado workers face a 4.4% state income tax on top of federal income tax and FICA. This guide shows exactly how much you take home at every income level in Colorado, compares cost of living across major cities, and explains what makes Colorado a higher-tax state for employees.

Tax Strategies for Colorado Workers

Regardless of Colorado's tax rules, there are key strategies to maximize your take-home pay and reduce your overall tax burden:

  • Max 401(k) to reduce CO taxable income: Colorado follows federal retirement account rules. At the flat 4.4% state rate, $23,500 in traditional 401(k) contributions saves $1,034 in Colorado state tax, plus federal savings.
  • Claim the Colorado Child Care Contribution Credit: Colorado offers a 50% tax credit (up to $100,000 in contributions) for qualified child care facility donations — a unique state-level opportunity for business owners and high earners.
  • Use Colorado's TABOR refund: Colorado's Taxpayer's Bill of Rights (TABOR) sometimes requires the state to refund excess revenue to taxpayers. In recent years, this has returned $400–$800 per taxpayer. Check Colorado.gov for current year status.
  • Consider Denver's local taxes: Denver city workers pay an Occupational Privilege Tax ($5.75/month employee + $4/month employer). Small but worth knowing. Some neighboring cities (Aurora, Lakewood) have similar local taxes.
  • Watch Denver housing costs: Denver median home price (~$550,000) has risen sharply. On a $70,000 salary, the 28% housing rule suggests a max mortgage payment of ~$1,633/month — limiting options in metro Denver without a large down payment.
