California Salary After Tax 2026

State Tax Guide January 2026 8 min read

California workers face a 9.3% state income tax on top of federal income tax and FICA. This guide shows exactly how much you take home at every income level in California, compares cost of living across major cities, and explains what makes California a higher-tax state for employees.

Tax Strategies for California Workers

Regardless of California's tax rules, there are key strategies to maximize your take-home pay and reduce your overall tax burden:

  • Max your 401(k): Contributing the full $23,500 to a traditional 401(k) reduces your California taxable income by $23,500 — saving up to $3,120 in state tax alone at the 13.3% top rate, plus federal tax savings.
  • Use an HSA if eligible: HSA contributions ($4,300 single / $8,550 family in 2026) are deductible for federal tax. Note: California does NOT recognize HSA deductions for state tax purposes — one of only two states with this rule.
  • Consider an FSA for healthcare: Flexible Spending Accounts reduce federal taxable income and FICA. California follows federal FSA rules, unlike HSAs — FSA contributions do lower your CA taxable income.
  • Track remote work days if working across state lines: If you work remotely for a non-California employer, days worked outside CA may not be subject to CA income tax. Keep records of work location for multi-state situations.
  • Itemize if mortgage interest is high: California's high home prices mean mortgage interest deductions can exceed the federal standard deduction for homeowners — making itemizing worthwhile for many CA workers.

✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
California's top marginal rate hits 13.3% — the highest state income tax in the US — but the effective rate for most earners is significantly lower. A single filer at $80,000 pays roughly 5.5% effective CA state tax in 2026. The SDI (State Disability Insurance) rate is 1.1% with no wage cap, which is new since 2024. For workers considering a California move for a salary bump, the rule of thumb is rough: you need a ~15% raise to match the take-home of a zero-tax state at the same gross salary above $100k.
