💵 What is Net Pay?

Paycheck GuideFebruary 202627 min read

Net pay is the amount deposited into your bank account after all taxes and deductions are subtracted from your gross wages. It is also called take-home pay, net wages, or net income. Understanding the difference between gross and net pay helps you budget accurately, negotiate salaries, and make smarter financial decisions.

Net Pay Definition

Net pay = Gross pay − All deductions (taxes + voluntary deductions)

For most US workers, net pay is 65–80% of gross pay. The exact percentage depends on:

  • Your federal income tax bracket
  • Your state income tax rate (0% in TX/FL to 13.3% in CA)
  • Pre-tax deductions (401k, health insurance, HSA)
  • Filing status (single vs married)

Net Pay vs Gross Pay vs Taxable Income

  • Gross pay: Total earnings before any deductions
  • Taxable income: Gross pay minus pre-tax deductions and standard/itemized deduction — this is what your tax rate applies to
  • Net pay: What you actually receive after all taxes and deductions

How to Increase Your Net Pay

  • Maximize pre-tax deductions — 401(k) and HSA contributions reduce taxable income, cutting your tax bill
  • Adjust W-4 withholding — if you consistently get large refunds, you are over-withholding (giving the IRS an interest-free loan)
  • Claim all eligible credits — Earned Income Credit, Child Tax Credit, education credits
  • Choose the right filing status — married filing jointly almost always produces a lower tax bill

Net Pay vs Taxable Income vs Adjusted Gross Income

These three terms are often confused. Here is exactly how they differ:

Key Income Terms Defined
Gross payTotal earnings before anything is deducted
Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)Gross pay minus above-the-line deductions (IRA, student loan interest, HSA). Used on your tax return.
Taxable incomeAGI minus standard or itemized deduction. This is what your tax brackets apply to.
Net pay (take-home)What hits your bank account after all withholding and deductions each pay period.

Your W-2 net pay and your tax return net income can differ — W-2 withholding is an estimate, and your actual tax liability is calculated when you file. If too much was withheld, you get a refund. If too little, you owe.

Why Your Net Pay Changes Mid-Year

Several situations cause your take-home to change without a pay raise:

  • Social Security wage base reached: Once your earnings hit $176,100 (2026), Social Security withholding (6.2%) stops for the rest of the year — giving you a notable take-home increase.
  • 401(k) contribution limit reached: If you max out your 401(k) ($23,500), those deductions stop and your net pay increases for the remaining pay periods.
  • FSA/HSA balance exhausted: If your FSA is fully used, the payroll deduction stops.
  • W-4 changes: Getting married, having a child, or taking a second job requires updating your W-4, which changes your withholding.
  • Health insurance premium changes: Annual open enrollment may change your premium deductions effective January 1.

Net Pay by Filing Status — The Difference is Significant

Filing status is one of the most impactful factors on your net pay, especially at higher incomes. Here is how net pay differs for a $80,000 salary:

Frequently Asked Questions

What is net pay? +
Net pay is your take-home pay — the amount deposited in your bank account after federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security, Medicare, and any voluntary deductions (401k, health insurance) are subtracted from your gross wages.
Is net pay the same as take-home pay? +
Yes — net pay, take-home pay, net wages, and net income all refer to the same thing: your earnings after all taxes and deductions are subtracted. The amount on your paycheck or direct deposit is your net pay.
What percentage of gross pay is net pay? +
For most US workers, net pay is 65–80% of gross pay. At $60,000 gross in a no-tax state, take-home is roughly 79% ($47,600). In a high-tax state like California, it may be closer to 68–72% of gross.
How is net pay calculated? +
Net pay = Gross pay minus pre-tax deductions (401k, HSA, health insurance) minus federal income tax minus FICA (7.65%) minus state and local income tax minus post-tax deductions. Use our Salary Calculator for an accurate step-by-step breakdown.
✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
Net pay confusion is at an all-time high in 2026 — partly because more workers have multiple deduction lines they don't recognize. Beyond federal and state income tax withholding and FICA, many paychecks now include: HSA contributions, FSA contributions, state-specific payroll taxes (WA Cares, CO FAMLI, CA SDI, NY PFML), supplemental life insurance premiums, and 401(k) loan repayments. None of these are errors — but they can easily make your net pay $200–$500/month lower than a simple tax calculator suggests. If your actual paycheck is significantly below what online calculators predict, look at your pre-tax benefit deductions first.
