Federal vs State Income Tax: What's Actually Taken from Your Paycheck

Tax Guide January 2026 11 min read

Most workers know their paycheck is smaller than their hourly rate implies — but few know exactly why. This guide breaks down every deduction that appears on a US paycheck: federal income tax, Social Security, Medicare, state income tax, and others — with real examples for three income levels.

The Five Major Paycheck Deductions

A typical US paycheck has five categories of deductions:

  1. Federal income tax — the largest and most variable deduction
  2. Social Security tax — 6.2% of gross wages, up to $176,100
  3. Medicare tax — 1.45% of all gross wages (no cap)
  4. State income tax — 0% to 13.3% depending on state
  5. Voluntary deductions — 401(k), health insurance, FSA, HSA

Items 2 and 3 together are commonly called FICA (Federal Insurance Contributions Act) taxes. They're non-negotiable — everyone who earns wages pays them, regardless of filing status or income level (below the SS wage cap).

Federal Income Tax: How the Brackets Work

The US uses a progressive tax system, meaning higher income is taxed at higher rates — but only the dollars within each bracket are taxed at that bracket's rate. Your "tax bracket" refers to your highest marginal rate, not the rate applied to all your income.

Example: A single filer earning $60,000 does not pay 22% on the full $60,000. After the $15,000 standard deduction, taxable income is $45,000. They pay: 10% on the first $11,925 ($1,193) + 12% on the next $33,075 ($3,969) = $5,161 total federal income tax. Their effective federal rate is 8.6% of gross pay — not 22%.

FICA: Social Security and Medicare

FICA taxes are flat rates applied to all earned income, with no deductions or credits:

  • Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $176,100 (2026 wage base). Your employer matches this 6.2%, contributing an additional 6.2% that you never see. Self-employed individuals pay both halves — 12.4% total.
  • Medicare: 1.45% on all wages, with no cap. An additional 0.9% Medicare surtax applies to wages above $200,000 (single filers). Employers match the base 1.45% but not the surtax.

Combined employee FICA rate: 7.65% for most workers. This is the same regardless of your state, your filing status, or your deductions.

State Income Tax: The Wild Card

State income tax varies enormously. Nine states have no state income tax at all; several others have rates above 9%. This dramatically affects your real take-home pay.

The Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing

Before federal income tax is calculated, you reduce your gross income by either the standard deduction or your itemized deductions — whichever is higher.

  • Standard deduction (2026): $15,000 for single filers, $30,000 for married filing jointly. This is a flat reduction with no documentation required.
  • Itemized deductions: The sum of specific deductible expenses — mortgage interest, state and local taxes (SALT, capped at $10,000), charitable contributions, medical expenses above 7.5% of AGI, and others.

Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act doubled the standard deduction, roughly 87% of US taxpayers now take the standard deduction. Itemizing only makes sense if your qualifying expenses exceed $15,000 (single) — which typically requires significant mortgage interest, very high SALT payments, or large charitable giving.

W-4 and Withholding: Why Your Paycheck Amount Changes

Your employer uses your W-4 form to determine how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. If your withholding is too high, you get a large refund at tax time (an interest-free loan to the government). If it's too low, you owe at filing and may face an underpayment penalty.

Common reasons take-home pay changes mid-year:

  • You update your W-4 (adding or removing dependents, claiming additional withholding)
  • Your income changes significantly (a raise, a second job, or unemployment)
  • You hit the Social Security wage base cap ($176,100) — Social Security withholding stops, increasing your paycheck
  • Your employer's health insurance premium changes at annual enrollment

Pre-Tax vs Post-Tax Deductions

Not all paycheck deductions are equal — some reduce your taxable income (pre-tax), others do not (post-tax):

  • Pre-tax (reduce taxable income): Traditional 401(k) contributions, health insurance premiums (employer-sponsored), HSA contributions, FSA contributions, commuter benefits. Contributing to these reduces your federal and state income tax.
  • Post-tax (do not reduce taxable income): Roth 401(k) contributions, union dues, wage garnishments, charitable payroll deductions. These come out after taxes are calculated.

Maximizing pre-tax contributions is one of the most accessible tax-reduction strategies. A worker earning $60,000 who contributes $10,000 to a traditional 401(k) reduces federal taxable income to $35,000 — saving approximately $1,200 in federal tax, $500 in state tax (varies), and building retirement wealth simultaneously.

💡 Key insight: The difference between your gross pay and net pay is not simply "taxes." It includes pre-tax deductions (which reduce your tax burden) and post-tax deductions. Understanding which category each deduction falls into helps you optimize your paycheck strategy.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between federal and state income tax? +
Federal income tax is collected by the IRS and applies to all US workers using graduated brackets (10–37%). State income tax is collected by individual states — 9 states have no income tax, while California's top rate reaches 13.3%. Both are withheld from your paycheck and calculated separately.
Why is my paycheck less than my salary? +
Your paycheck is reduced by federal income tax (10–37% marginal rate), FICA taxes (7.65% for Social Security and Medicare), and state income tax (0–13.3%). Pre-tax deductions like 401(k) and health insurance also reduce your check, though they lower your taxable income.
What states have no income tax? +
Nine states have no individual income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (on wages), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Living in a no-tax state can mean $2,000–$8,000+ more in take-home pay annually depending on your income.
