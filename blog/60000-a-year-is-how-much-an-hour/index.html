$60,000 a Year is How Much an Hour?

Salary GuideJune 20268 min read

$60,000/year ÷ 2,080 hours = $28.85/hour before taxes. After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer in a no-tax state takes home approximately $49,900/year ($4,158/month). This guide breaks down every pay period, after-tax pay by state, and what $60,000/year actually means for your budget in 2026.

Is $60,000 a Year a Good Salary in 2026?

$60,000/year ($28.85/hour) is close to the US median household income and above the individual earnings median. Whether it's "good" depends heavily on location and household size.

  • Comfortable: Most Midwest and Southern cities — Columbus, Indianapolis, Kansas City, Memphis, San Antonio. Homeownership is achievable, savings are realistic.
  • Manageable with discipline: Mid-tier metros like Denver, Austin, Nashville, Charlotte. Renting is fine; buying requires careful planning.
  • Tight: High-cost metros — San Francisco, NYC, Seattle, Los Angeles, Boston. $60k is a difficult income in these markets without roommates or dual income.

Using the 30% housing rule, $60,000/year supports rent up to $1,500/month. Targeting 10% savings yields $417/month.

How to Move from $60,000 to $80,000

The jump from $60k to $80k adds roughly $1,300/month in take-home pay in a no-tax state — meaningful enough to change your financial trajectory. The most realistic paths:

  • Strategic employer move: Switching companies is the fastest path at this income level — typically yielding 15–25% salary increases versus 3–5% merit raises internally.
  • Targeted skill upgrade: Adding a high-value certification in your field (PMP, AWS, CPA, trade license) often unlocks the $75k–$85k range in 12–18 months.
  • Negotiate at your next review: Come in with market data — use our salary negotiation guide and comp benchmarks from LinkedIn Salary and Glassdoor. At $60k, you're often underpaid relative to market without realizing it.

✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
$60,000/year is an interesting income point in 2026 because it sits right at the US median — which means half of full-time workers earn less, and half earn more. The honest reality is that $60k feels very different depending on where you live. In cities like Columbus, Omaha, or Knoxville, it's a genuinely comfortable individual income — you can rent a decent apartment, save a real amount, and live without constant financial stress. In coastal metros, it's a survival income that requires roommates or significant lifestyle compromises. If you're at $60k and feeling stretched, geography is often the most powerful lever: the same salary goes 30–40% further in a lower-cost market, and remote work has made that relocation option accessible for more knowledge workers than at any prior point.
