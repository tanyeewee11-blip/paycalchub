🧾 Gross Pay vs Net Pay

Paycheck GuideFebruary 202629 min read

Gross pay is what your employer agrees to pay you. Net pay — your 'take-home' — is what actually lands in your bank account after taxes and deductions. The gap between the two surprises many workers: on a $60,000 salary, you might take home only $46,000–$50,000 depending on your state and benefits elections. This guide explains exactly what gets deducted, why, and how to calculate your real take-home pay.

What is Gross Pay?

Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions. For salaried workers, it is your annual salary divided by the number of pay periods. For hourly workers, it is your hourly rate multiplied by hours worked, including any overtime.

Gross Pay Examples
$60,000 salaried, bi-weekly (26 pays)$2,307.69/paycheck gross
$25/hour, 40 hours/week$1,000/week gross
$25/hour, 45 hours (5 hrs OT at 1.5x)$1,187.50/week gross

What is Net Pay?

Net pay (take-home pay) is what remains after all deductions are subtracted from gross pay. Deductions fall into two categories:

  • Mandatory deductions: Federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%)
  • Voluntary deductions: 401(k) contributions, health insurance premiums, HSA/FSA contributions, life insurance, dental/vision

Step-by-Step: How Net Pay is Calculated

1

Start with Gross Pay

Your total earnings before anything is deducted. For a $60,000 salary paid bi-weekly: $2,307.69/paycheck.

2

Subtract Pre-Tax Deductions

401(k), HSA, and health insurance premiums are deducted before taxes are calculated — reducing your taxable income. Example: $500/month 401(k) = $6,000/year less in taxable income.

3

Calculate Federal Income Tax

Applied to taxable income (gross minus pre-tax deductions minus standard deduction). Uses graduated brackets — 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%.

4

Subtract FICA Taxes

Social Security: 6.2% on wages up to $176,100. Medicare: 1.45% on all wages. Combined: 7.65% employee share.

5

Subtract State & Local Taxes

Varies by state — 0% in TX/FL/WA to 13.3% in California. Some cities add local income tax (NYC: 3.08–3.88%).

6

Subtract Post-Tax Deductions

Roth 401(k) contributions, some insurance types, and wage garnishments come out after taxes.

How Pre-Tax Deductions Reduce Your Tax Bill

One of the most powerful ways to increase net pay is to maximize pre-tax deductions. Every dollar contributed to a traditional 401(k) or HSA reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar:

Tax Savings from Pre-Tax Contributions (22% Bracket)
$5,000 401(k) contributionSaves ~$1,100 in federal tax + FICA
$3,000 HSA contributionSaves ~$660 in federal tax + FICA
$3,000 health insurance premiumSaves ~$660 in federal tax + FICA
Max 401(k) ($23,500) + HSA ($4,300)Saves ~$6,100+ in federal tax + FICA

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between gross and net pay? +
Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions. Net pay (take-home pay) is what remains after federal and state income taxes, FICA (Social Security and Medicare), and any voluntary deductions like 401(k) or health insurance are subtracted.
Why is my net pay so much less than my salary? +
The gap between gross and net pay is typically 20–35% for most US workers. Federal income tax (10–37%), FICA (7.65%), and state income tax (0–13.3%) together take the largest share. Pre-tax benefit deductions (health insurance, 401k) further reduce your check but also reduce your taxable income.
How do I calculate my net pay? +
Start with gross pay, subtract pre-tax deductions (401k, health insurance, HSA), then subtract federal income tax (using IRS brackets), FICA (7.65%), and state/local income tax. Use our Salary Calculator for an instant and accurate calculation.
Can I increase my net pay without a raise? +
Yes — adjusting your W-4 withholding, maximizing pre-tax deductions (401k, HSA, FSA), and ensuring you are claiming all eligible tax credits can all increase your net pay without a salary increase.
✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
One thing that surprises many new workers: FICA (Social Security + Medicare) taxes come out before your income tax withholding calculation, and they have no deductions or exemptions. Every dollar of gross pay up to $176,100 (2026 Social Security wage base) gets hit with 7.65% FICA regardless of your filing status, deductions, or credits. This is why your effective total tax rate feels higher than your income tax bracket alone — FICA is a flat, unavoidable slice off the top.
