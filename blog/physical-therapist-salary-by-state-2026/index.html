🦴 Physical Therapist (PT) Salary 2026

Career & Salary June 2026 9 min read

Physical therapists earn a median salary of $99,710/year ($47.94/hour) nationally in 2026, according to BLS data — putting the profession solidly in the top 25% of US earners. But pay varies significantly by state, setting, and experience level: from around $72,000 for new DPT graduates to $130,000+ for experienced PTs in high-demand markets. This guide covers every level of PT pay, after-tax take-home by state, and how the PTA vs PT salary gap plays out in practice.

Job Outlook & Growth

The BLS projects physical therapist employment to grow 15% from 2022 to 2032 — more than three times the average for all occupations. This is driven by an aging Baby Boomer population, increased rates of chronic conditions requiring rehabilitation, and expanded insurance coverage for PT services under the ACA.

Approximately 23,800 new PT positions are projected to open each year through 2032, a combination of growth and replacement of retiring PTs. Job security in this profession is exceptionally strong — PT unemployment rates consistently run below 1.5%.

Education & Licensing Requirements

Becoming a physical therapist requires completing an accredited Doctor of Physical Therapy (DPT) program — typically three years following a bachelor's degree — and passing the NPTE (National Physical Therapy Examination). All states require licensure; some have additional continuing education requirements for renewal.

The main financial consideration for prospective PTs: DPT programs are expensive. Average total program cost (tuition plus fees) runs $130,000–$180,000 at private programs and $80,000–$120,000 at in-state public programs. At a starting salary of $72,000, loan-to-income ratios are manageable — but worth modeling before committing to a program.

💡 Student debt tip: Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) is a significant benefit for PTs working in hospitals, government facilities, VA clinics, or school districts — all of which qualify as public service employers. After 10 years of qualifying payments, remaining federal loan balances are forgiven tax-free. For a PT with $150,000 in loans at a government salary of $95,000, PSLF can result in $60,000–$80,000 in forgiven debt versus standard repayment.

Is Physical Therapy Worth the Degree Cost?

At a median of $99,710 and with 15% job growth projected, physical therapy compares favorably to other healthcare careers requiring similar education investment. The DPT takes 7 years total (4-year bachelor's + 3-year DPT), which is less than medicine (11+ years) and roughly equivalent to pharmacy or optometry — but PT median pay runs below those fields.

The strongest case for PT: job flexibility. PTs work in outpatient clinics, hospitals, schools, home health, sports teams, research, and private practice. Specialty certifications (orthopedic, neurologic, sports, pediatric) open higher-paying niches. Travel PT — similar to travel nursing — remains available for PTs willing to take 13-week contracts, often at $2,200–$3,500/week.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average physical therapist salary in 2026? +
The median physical therapist salary is $99,710/year ($47.94/hour) nationally in 2026. Pay ranges from approximately $72,000 for new DPT graduates to $130,000+ for experienced or specialized PTs in high-demand states.
Which state pays physical therapists the most? +
Nevada pays the highest PT salaries at a median of approximately $116,000/year, followed by California ($112,000), New Jersey ($110,000), and Alaska ($108,000). Cost of living varies significantly across these states, so after-tax purchasing power doesn't always follow gross salary rankings.
How much does a PTA make compared to a PT? +
Physical therapist assistants (PTAs) earn a median of $63,000/year — about 63% of a PT's median salary. PTAs require a two-year associate degree versus the three-year Doctor of Physical Therapy (DPT) required for licensed PTs. The salary gap is meaningful, but the shorter education path and lower debt load changes the financial calculus for many.
Is physical therapy a good career financially? +
Yes, for most people. PTs earn well above the US median, 15% job growth is projected through 2032, and the career offers strong flexibility across settings. The main risk is student debt — DPT programs average $130,000–$180,000 in cost. PTs working in qualifying public service settings should seriously evaluate PSLF, which can eliminate a significant portion of federal loans after 10 years.
Do physical therapists get overtime? +
It depends on the setting. Salaried PTs in private outpatient clinics often don't receive overtime pay. PTs in hospitals or SNFs working hourly schedules typically do. Travel PTs generally work set-hour contracts with overtime built into their rate. Home health PTs paid per visit have an effectively unlimited earning ceiling — and a high-productivity visit schedule can push annual earnings well above $120,000 in many markets.
✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
Physical therapy is at an interesting inflection point in 2026. Demand is structurally strong — the aging population isn't going anywhere — but the profession is navigating two headwinds simultaneously. First, private equity consolidation of outpatient PT chains has put downward pressure on per-visit reimbursement rates, which squeezes clinic margins and can limit salary growth for staff PTs. Second, Medicare reimbursement rates for PT services have been cut repeatedly over the past decade, and 2026 brought another modest reduction. The PTs seeing the strongest compensation growth are those in home health, cash-pay practices (which bypass insurance entirely), and specialized sports or concussion clinics. If you're evaluating PT as a career, the setting you choose may matter more than the state you're in.
