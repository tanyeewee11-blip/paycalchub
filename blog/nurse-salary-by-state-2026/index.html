🏥 Registered Nurse (RN) Salary 2026

Career & Salary January 2026 9 min read

Registered nurses earn a median salary of $81,220/year ($39.05/hour) nationally in 2026, according to BLS data. But pay varies enormously by state, specialty, and experience — from $58,000 for new graduates in rural areas to $130,000+ for experienced RNs in California or Alaska. This guide covers every level of nursing pay, after-tax take-home, and the best states for nursing salaries.

Job Outlook & Growth

The BLS projects registered nursing jobs to grow 6% from 2022–2032, adding approximately 177,400 positions. Demand is driven by an aging population, increased chronic disease prevalence, and retiring Baby Boomer nurses. Job security is among the strongest of any profession — RN unemployment rates consistently run below 2%.

Education & Qualifications

Registered nurses require either an Associate Degree in Nursing (ADN, 2 years) or a Bachelor of Science in Nursing (BSN, 4 years). BSN-prepared nurses earn 10–15% more on average and have better advancement prospects. Nurse practitioners (NPs) require a Master's degree and earn a median of $124,000/year. LPNs (Licensed Practical Nurses) require a 1-year diploma and earn a median of $54,000/year.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average RN salary in 2026? +
The median registered nurse salary is $81,220/year ($39.05/hour) nationally in 2026. Pay ranges from ~$58,000 for entry-level nurses to $130,000+ for experienced RNs in high-paying states like California.
Which state pays nurses the most? +
California pays the highest RN salaries at a median of ~$124,000/year, followed by Hawaii ($113k), Oregon ($106k), and Alaska ($102k). These states also have higher costs of living, so purchasing power varies.
How much do travel nurses make? +
Travel nurses (RNs working 13-week contracts through agencies) typically earn $2,000–$3,500/week including tax-free stipends for housing and meals. Annual equivalent: $100,000–$180,000. Travel pay varies significantly by specialty and location demand.
Is nursing a good career financially? +
Yes. Nursing offers strong job security (6% growth projected), above-median pay starting at entry level, overtime opportunities, and a clear career ladder from LPN to RN to NP with significant salary increases at each step.
✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
The nurse salary landscape in 2026 has normalized somewhat from the 2021–2022 travel nursing boom, when some contracts paid $8,000–$12,000/week. Travel rates have come down 30–40% from those peaks, but permanent RN salaries have risen meaningfully in most markets as hospitals tried to compete. California remains the highest-paying state for staff nurses (median $130k+), but the cost of living premium is significant. One trend: hospital systems are aggressively pushing mandatory overtime and patient ratios, making total compensation (including burnout risk) an important factor beyond base salary comparisons.
