$22 an Hour is How Much a Year?

Salary Guide January 2026 8 min read

$22/hour × 2,080 hours = $45,760/year before taxes. After federal income tax and FICA, a single filer in a no-tax state takes home approximately $38,807/year ($3,234/month). This guide breaks down every pay period, after-tax pay by state, and a realistic monthly budget.

Is $22 an Hour a Good Wage?

At $22/hr, a single adult can live comfortably in most US cities and begin building savings and retirement contributions.

For context, the federal minimum wage is $7.25/hour ($45,760 annualized vs. $15,080 for minimum wage). The US median individual wage is approximately $28/hour ($59,000/year) as of 2026. At $22/hr, you are below the national median.

Frequently Asked Questions

$22 an hour is how much a year? +
$22 per hour equals $45,760 per year, assuming 40 hours per week and 52 weeks (2,080 hours). Working 50 weeks gives $44,000/year; part-time at 20 hours/week gives $22,880/year.
What is the take-home pay for $22 an hour? +
A single filer earning $22/hr takes home approximately $38,807/year or $3,234/month after federal income tax and FICA, in a state with no income tax. With state taxes, take-home is lower — typically $36,519–$38,807/year depending on your state.
Is $22 an hour a good wage? +
$22/hr is below the US median individual wage of ~$28/hour (~$59,000/year). At $22/hr, a single adult can live comfortably in most US cities and begin building savings and retirement contributions.
How much is $22 an hour monthly? +
At $22/hour, your gross monthly income is $3,813.33 ($$45,760/12). After federal taxes and FICA, take-home is approximately $3,234/month for a single filer with no state income tax.
✎ Editor's Note — June 2026
$22/hr ($45,760/year gross) sits just above the US median individual income, but the gap between states is stark in 2026. In no-income-tax states like Texas or Florida, you keep roughly $36,500 after federal taxes. In California, that drops closer to $33,800. For workers at this income level, contributing even 3–5% to a 401(k) with employer match is often the single highest-return financial move available — don't leave that match on the table.
