How Much House Can You Afford on $70,000?

On $70,000 a year, your gross monthly income is $5,833. Lenders typically use the 28/36 rule to assess affordability: your monthly housing costs (mortgage principal, interest, property taxes, and insurance — called PITI) should not exceed 28% of gross monthly income, which is $1,633/month. Your total monthly debt payments (housing + car, student loans, etc.) should not exceed 36%, or $2,100/month.

Based on these guidelines and a current mortgage rate of approximately 6.9%, a $70,000 salary can support a home price between $215,000 (3.5% down, conservative DTI) and $322,000 (20% down, higher DTI). With a 20% down payment and no existing debts, your comfortable range is around $251,000.

What Affects How Much House You Can Afford?

Credit score — A higher credit score (740+) typically qualifies you for lower mortgage rates, meaningfully increasing buying power. A 0.5% rate difference on a $228,000 home changes your monthly payment by roughly $67.

Existing debt — Car loans, student loans, and credit card minimums reduce how much mortgage payment you can qualify for. Even $300/month in existing debt reduces your max home price by roughly $46,000.

Down payment — A larger down payment reduces your loan amount and eliminates PMI (private mortgage insurance) once you reach 20%. PMI typically costs 0.5–1.5% of the loan amount annually, adding $152/month on a $228,000 home with 10% down.

Mortgage rate — At 6.9% on a 30-year fixed mortgage, monthly payments on a $228,000 home with 20% down are approximately $1,201. If rates were 1% lower, the same home would cost $1,082/month — a difference of $119/month.

How Much Do You Need for a Down Payment on $70,000?

On $70,000 a year, your realistic down payment options are:

  • 3.5% (FHA loan) — $7,525 on a $215,000 home. Requires a minimum 580 credit score.
  • 5% (Conventional) — $11,400 on a $228,000 home. PMI required until you reach 20% equity.
  • 20% (No PMI) — $50,200 on a $251,000 home. Eliminates PMI and reduces monthly payments significantly.

Saving for a down payment on $70,000 requires careful planning. Setting aside 20% of net income ($957/month) would take approximately 26 months to reach a 10% down payment on a $251,000 home.

Tips to Maximize Buying Power on $70,000

  • Pay down existing debt before applying — every $100/month in debt payments eliminated can increase your max home price by roughly $15,000–$20,000.
  • Improve your credit score — moving from 680 to 740+ can reduce your rate by 0.25–0.5%, saving tens of thousands over the loan term.
  • Consider first-time buyer programs — many states offer down payment assistance grants and low-rate loans for incomes at this level.
  • Shop multiple lenders — rate differences of 0.25–0.5% between lenders are common, and can save $34 or more per month.
